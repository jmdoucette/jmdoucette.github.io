Random Selection Games

While camping over the summer, me and two friends came across a situation where needed to each carry one of distinct, differently sized objects. To settle this fairly we decided to first play a three player round of rock paper scissors and then a regular round. A major problem we came across was the high chance of ties.

This properties of the potential for ties is certainly a drawback. I remembered from a game theory class a game called matcher avoider. One player is chosen to be the matcher and the other player is chosen to be the avoider. Each Player holds up either 0 or 1 fingers behind their back. The matcher wins if the both players choose the same number of fingers and the avoider wins if the players choose different numbers of fingers.

We thought a bit more about this a realized the matcher avoider game could be seen as a special case of a more general game of this type for players. Have all players secretly pick a number between 0 and n-1 (on their fingers if the number of players is small enough). Add up the numbers chosen by all players modulo n. A quick check can show that all players have an equal chance of winning and that no option is better than the others.

While we had shown that a players number does not impact their chance of success, a skeptical player may believe they are being tricked. Unfortunately a quick counterexample shows that uniformity and the impossibility of a tie are mutually exclusive. If all players choose the same option, uniformity means the same thing must happen to them all.
